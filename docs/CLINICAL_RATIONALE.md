## ğŸ”‹ Energy & Fatigue - Clinical Reasoning

### Why These Questions Matter

Energy and fatigue complaints are among the most common in primary care but also the most diagnostically challenging. They can represent everything from simple sleep debt to serious systemic disease.

### Question Rationale

#### **Fatigue Severity** - Primary Differentiator
- **Normal tired vs Pathological fatigue**: This immediately separates lifestyle issues from medical conditions
- **"Exhausted even after sleeping"** â†’ Points to sleep disorders (apnea), metabolic issues (thyroid, diabetes), or chronic fatigue syndrome
- **"Sudden crashes"** â†’ Suggests adrenal issues, post-exertional malaise (CFS/ME hallmark), or blood sugar problems
- **"Constant drain"** â†’ Indicates chronic conditions: anemia, cancer, autoimmune disease

#### **Activity Capacity** - Functional Assessment
- **Percentage-based measurement** provides objective disability assessment
- **<25% capacity** â†’ Severe impairment requiring urgent evaluation
- **25-50%** â†’ Moderate disease burden, likely systemic condition
- **50-75%** â†’ Mild-moderate, could be early disease or deconditioning
- Used for disability documentation and treatment response monitoring

#### **Associated Symptoms** - Pattern Recognition
- **Muscle weakness + fatigue** â†’ Neuromuscular diseases, myasthenia gravis
- **Brain fog + unrefreshing sleep** â†’ Classic CFS/ME or fibromyalgia
- **Body aches + fatigue** â†’ Viral, autoimmune, or fibromyalgia
- Combination patterns help narrow differential quickly

### Red Flag Patterns
- Fatigue + unintended weight loss â†’ Cancer screening needed
- Fatigue + night sweats â†’ Lymphoma, TB, HIV screening
- Sudden severe fatigue in elderly â†’ Cardiac evaluation

## ğŸ§  Mental Health - Clinical Reasoning

### Why These Questions Matter

Mental health presentations often involve stigma and reluctance to disclose. Questions must be non-threatening while capturing DSM-5 criteria and functional impact.

### Question Rationale

#### **Main Difficulty** - Functional Domains
Maps directly to DSM-5 symptom clusters:
- **Energy/motivation** â†’ Major depression cardinal symptom
- **Mood/emotions** â†’ Mood disorder spectrum
- **Worry/thoughts** â†’ Anxiety disorders, OCD, psychosis screening
- **Sleep/appetite** â†’ Neurovegetative symptoms (depression severity marker)
- **Memory/concentration** â†’ Cognitive symptoms (severe depression, ADHD, dementia)
- **Physical symptoms** â†’ Somatization, panic disorder
- **Relationships** â†’ Social impact (all disorders)

#### **Symptom Description** - Unstructured Insight
- Free text reveals thought organization (or disorganization)
- Language patterns indicate severity (hopelessness phrases)
- Helps identify suicidal ideation without direct questioning
- Shows insight level (awareness of problem)

#### **Duration Pattern** - Diagnostic Criteria
- **Days** â†’ Adjustment disorder, acute stress
- **Weeks** â†’ Major depressive episode (2+ weeks required)
- **Months** â†’ Persistent depressive disorder
- **Years** â†’ Personality factors, chronic conditions
- **Episodic** â†’ Bipolar screening critical

### Red Flag Patterns
- New onset in elderly â†’ Medical cause likely (thyroid, B12, dementia)
- Sudden personality change â†’ Brain tumor, infection
- Physical symptoms prominent â†’ Medical workup needed

## ğŸ¤’ Feeling Sick - Clinical Reasoning

### Why These Questions Matter

Acute illness requires rapid triage to identify who needs immediate care versus home management. These questions separate viral syndromes from bacterial infections and serious conditions.

### Question Rationale

#### **Onset Speed** - Pathogen Identification
- **Sudden (hours)** â†’ Bacterial (strep, pneumonia), allergic reaction
- **Quick (1-2 days)** â†’ Typical viral (flu, COVID)
- **Gradual (week)** â†’ Atypical infections, early systemic disease
- **Slow (weeks+)** â†’ Chronic infections (TB), malignancy

#### **Main Symptoms** - Syndrome Recognition
Combinations identify specific syndromes:
- **Fever + cough + body aches** â†’ Influenza-like illness
- **Sore throat + fever without cough** â†’ Streptococcal pharyngitis
- **Stomach issues + fever** â†’ Gastroenteritis
- **Headache + fever + neck stiffness** â†’ Meningitis concern

#### **Severity Progression** - Treatment Urgency
- **Worse quickly** â†’ Bacterial infection, sepsis risk
- **Improving** â†’ Viral, self-limited
- **Fluctuating** â†’ Malaria pattern, complex infection

#### **Risk Factors** - Exposure History
- **Travel** â†’ Exotic infections, COVID variants
- **Sick contacts** â†’ Contagious period assessment
- **New medication** â†’ Drug reaction vs infection
- **Stress** â†’ Immune suppression consideration

### Red Flag Patterns
- Fever + confusion â†’ Sepsis, meningitis
- Fever + shortness of breath â†’ Pneumonia, PE
- Fever + severe headache â†’ Meningitis, encephalitis

## ğŸ’Š Medication Side Effects - Clinical Reasoning

### Why These Questions Matter

Medication side effects account for significant morbidity. Distinguishing true side effects from disease symptoms guides whether to continue, adjust, or stop medications.

### Question Rationale

#### **Which Medications** - Temporal Relationship
- **New medication (<1 month)** â†’ Most side effects occur early
- **Dose change** â†’ Dose-dependent toxicity
- **Long-term** â†’ Cumulative effects (kidney, liver)
- **Stopped medication** â†’ Withdrawal syndromes

#### **System Affected** - Drug Class Patterns
- **Stomach** â†’ NSAIDs, antibiotics, metformin
- **Dizziness** â†’ Blood pressure meds, benzos, antihistamines
- **Mood/sleep** â†’ Steroids, beta-blockers, stimulants
- **Sexual** â†’ SSRIs, blood pressure meds
Helps identify culprit medication in polypharmacy

#### **Effect on Life** - Management Decision
- **Tolerable** â†’ Continue with monitoring
- **Affecting daily** â†’ Dose adjustment, timing change
- **Unbearable** â†’ Medication change needed
- **Dangerous** â†’ Immediate discontinuation

### Red Flag Patterns
- Rash + fever + medication â†’ Stevens-Johnson syndrome
- Muscle pain + statins â†’ Rhabdomyolysis risk
- Confusion + elderly + new medication â†’ Delirium

### Red Flag Patterns

## ğŸ’ª Physical/Body - Clinical Reasoning

### Why These Questions Matter

Physical complaints are the most common presenting symptoms. Proper characterization separates benign musculoskeletal issues from serious pathology requiring immediate intervention.

### Question Rationale

#### **Issue Characteristics** (Adaptive)
Different issue types require different characterization:

**Pain patterns**:
- **Sharp with movement** â†’ Mechanical/musculoskeletal
- **Constant ache** â†’ Inflammatory or visceral
- **Burning/tingling** â†’ Neuropathic
- **Waves** â†’ Visceral (kidney stone, bowel)

**Weakness patterns**:
- **Can't lift** â†’ Proximal weakness (myopathy)
- **Legs give out** â†’ Neurological (cord, nerve)
- **One side** â†’ Stroke, nerve compression

#### **Mechanical Behavior** - Tissue Identification
- **Predictable with movement** â†’ Mechanical (muscle, joint, ligament)
- **Constant** â†’ Inflammatory (arthritis) or tumor
- **Worse at rest/night** â†’ Inflammatory or neoplastic (classic red flag)
- **Waves/attacks** â†’ Visceral or vascular

#### **Red Flag Screen** - Emergency Triage
Critical for identifying surgical emergencies:
- **Bladder/bowel loss** â†’ Cauda equina syndrome (surgical emergency)
- **Groin numbness** â†’ Saddle anesthesia (cord compression)
- **Fever + back pain** â†’ Spinal infection/abscess
- **Night pain** â†’ Tumor (primary or metastatic)

#### **What Helped** - Treatment Clues
- **Rest helps** â†’ Mechanical/overuse
- **Movement helps** â†’ Inflammatory (morning stiffness)
- **Heat helps** â†’ Muscle spasm
- **Ice helps** â†’ Acute inflammation
- **Nothing helps** â†’ Consider serious pathology

### Red Flag Patterns
- Back pain + fever + IV drug use â†’ Epidural abscess
- Progressive weakness + sensory loss â†’ Cord compression
- Unilateral leg swelling + pain â†’ DVT

## Integration with AI Analysis

### How the AI Should Use These Responses

1. **Pattern Matching**: Look for classic combinations (e.g., fatigue + joint pain + rash = lupus screen)

2. **Red Flag Priority**: Any red flag response triggers urgent care recommendation

3. **Confidence Scoring**: 
   - High confidence: Classic pattern with multiple supporting features
   - Medium confidence: Some supporting features but incomplete picture
   - Low confidence: Vague or contradictory responses

4. **Follow-up Generation**: AI should generate follow-up questions targeting:
   - Missing red flag screens
   - Clarification of ambiguous responses
   - Confirmation of suspected patterns

5. **Recommendation Hierarchy**:
   - Emergency: Red flags present
   - Urgent: Progressive symptoms, moderate functional impairment
   - Routine: Stable symptoms, minimal impairment
   - Self-care: Clear benign pattern, improving symptoms

## Quality Metrics

### What Makes a Good Clinical Question

1. **Discriminatory Power**: Separates multiple conditions efficiently
2. **Objective Measures**: Quantifiable when possible (percentages, time)
3. **Pattern Recognition**: Identifies known clinical syndromes
4. **Safety First**: Always screens for dangerous conditions
5. **Actionable**: Responses guide specific next steps

### Question Performance Metrics

Track these to improve questions over time:
- **Completion Rate**: Are users finishing all questions?
- **Diagnostic Accuracy**: Do answers lead to correct diagnosis?
- **Red Flag Capture**: Are serious conditions being caught?
- **User Satisfaction**: Do users feel heard and understood?
- **Clinical Utility**: Do healthcare providers find summaries useful?

## ğŸ« Breathing & Chest - Clinical Reasoning

### Why These Questions Matter

Dyspnea can represent immediately life-threatening conditions (MI, PE) or chronic diseases. Proper assessment determines urgency and likely organ system.

### Question Rationale

#### **Breathing Pattern** - System Identification
- **Can't catch breath** â†’ Air hunger (cardiac/metabolic)
- **Rapid shallow** â†’ Anxiety vs restrictive lung
- **Wheezing** â†’ Obstructive (asthma, COPD)
- **Worse lying down** â†’ Orthopnea (heart failure hallmark)

#### **Exertion Impact** - Severity Grading
Uses validated NYHA/MRC scales:
- **At rest** â†’ Class IV, severe disease
- **Minimal activity** â†’ Class III, needs urgent evaluation
- **Moderate activity** â†’ Class II, significant impairment
- **Strenuous only** â†’ Class I, mild disease

#### **Chest Symptoms** - Differential
- **Sharp pain** â†’ Pleuritic (PE, pneumonia) or musculoskeletal
- **Pressure/squeezing** â†’ Cardiac ischemia
- **Burning** â†’ GERD or cardiac
- **Palpitations** â†’ Arrhythmia or anxiety

#### **Urgent Screen** - Life Threats
Each symptom represents different emergency:
- **Chest pressure + sweating** â†’ MI
- **Blue lips** â†’ Severe hypoxia
- **Coughing blood** â†’ PE, cancer, TB
- **Leg swelling** â†’ DVT/PE or heart failure

### Red Flag Patterns
- Sudden onset â†’ PE, pneumothorax
- Chest pain + dyspnea + risk factors â†’ PE until proven otherwise
- Orthopnea + PND + edema â†’ Heart failure
